library(MASS)

boxcox(display$Sales ~ display$Display.Feet)

# Lambda tells us how to transform, if not 0, then we take y^lambda - 1 / lambda
# if y = 0, we take natural log of lambda
# From the plot, let's try lambda 1.5

powerModel <-  lm((Sales^1.5 -1 ) / 1.5 ~ Display.Feet, data = display)

summary(powerModel)

We can see after transforming our data, the relationship between Display Feet and product Sales has a much more linear relationship. However, now we must be more careful in our interpretation of slope and intercept. The intercept is what the \(\frac{sales^{1.5} - 1}{1.5}\) if we had now display space given to the product. Our slope would be how much a unit increase in display space in feet would increase \(\frac{sales^{1.5} - 1}{1.5}\).

We can see in both models that we find the slope to be significant at any reasonable \(\alpha\), leading us to reject a null hypothesis that our \(\beta_1\) is equal to zero. However, we notice that with our power model, that our slope is not significant any any p-value 0.05 or lower. We see that our adjusted \(R^2\) is slightly higher in our power transformation model than our original linear model. That is, under the power model, 71.4% of the variation in our transformed sales is explained by the display feet. We can say that our power transformation model more accurately fits our data than a simple linear model.
